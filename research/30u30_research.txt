1. DELVE — "FAKE COMPLIANCE AS A SERVICE" (2023–2026)
================================================================================

THE PITCH (the version told to investors and customers)
--------------------------------------------------------
Delve was an "AI-native" automation platform that promised to deliver SOC 2,
ISO 27001, HIPAA, and GDPR compliance certifications "in days, not months,"
using "AI agents" to collect evidence, write reports, and monitor compliance
gaps. Pricing was $6,000–$15,000 for packages that conventionally cost
six figures and took 6–12 months. The company claimed 1,500+ customers and
$32M Series A funding at a $300M valuation.

Tagline (literally on their website): "Delve gets you compliant in days,
not months."

THE PEOPLE
----------
- Karun Kaushik — CEO, MIT dropout, 21 when they raised the Series A.
  Forbes 30 Under 30 (2026 list).
- Selin Kocalar — COO, MIT dropout, 21. Also Forbes 30 Under 30 (2026).
- Both founded the company in 2023 from a dorm room. Graduated YC W24.

THE MONEY
---------
- $3M seed
- $32M Series A led by Insight Partners (July 2025)
- $300M valuation
- Other backers: General Catalyst, FundersClub, Soma Capital, Fortune 500 CISOs
- Customer list included: Lovable, Bland, Cluely, Notion, Brex, Anthropic,
  Gusto, NASDAQ-traded Duos Edge

THE BREAK
---------
December 2025: A whistleblower email circulates to hundreds of Delve clients
saying their audit reports were fabricated. Delve CEO Karun Kaushik tells
customers they're fine and no external party gained access.

March 18-22, 2026: An anonymous Substack writer using the handle "DeepDelver"
publishes "Delve – Fake Compliance as a Service – Part I." DeepDelver was
themselves a former Delve customer who had been pooling resources with other
suspicious customers to investigate.

KEY ALLEGATIONS (from the DeepDelver investigation)
---------------------------------------------------
- 493 out of 494 SOC 2 reports were 99.8% identical — same paragraphs, same
  grammatical errors, same exact phrasing across hundreds of clients.
  Only the customer name changed.
- Auditor reports and test results were fully populated BEFORE clients
  submitted their company descriptions, network diagrams, or any evidence
  — directly violating AICPA independence rules.
- Customers were routed through two audit firms — "Accorp" and "Gradient" —
  that DeepDelver said were "part of the same operation" operating primarily
  in India with only nominal U.S. presence ("certification mills").
- Delve's "Questionnaire AI" auto-answered 70% of vendor security questions
  with answers that were straight-up untrue (claims of MDM, 200-hour pen-tests,
  regular backup restoration simulations) — none of which had been performed.
- Delve hosted "trust pages" listing security measures that were never
  implemented.
- The "AI" was barely AI. DeepDelver: "It mostly felt like a SOC 2 template
  pack with a thin SAAS platform wrapper where you simply adopt and sign all
  templated documents."

PART II — THE SIM.AI THEFT
---------------------------
DeepDelver's Part II (~March 30, 2026) added an entirely separate scandal.

- Delve had a no-code workflow product called "Pathways" priced at $50K–$200K
  per seat that they marketed as built from the ground up.
- It was actually a fork of SimStudio, the open-source product (Apache 2.0)
  of Sim.ai — itself a YC company.
- Apache 2.0 requires attribution. Delve had stripped it.
- Sim.ai had been a paying Delve customer (paid $15K for SOC 2 + HIPAA
  packages, with Karun Kaushik personally handling onboarding).
- Internal Delve documents flagged SimStudio as "UI inspo for Pathways"
  during the Sim.ai sales call. Delve's production code reportedly still
  contained SimStudio references.
- Delve's response to Sim.ai when Sim asked about partnering: there's
  "no high enough ROI rn."
- Sim co-founder Emir Karabeg confirmed there had never been a license
  agreement. Quote: "Ultimately, it's open source so there's nothing we
  can really do about it. That's just part of having software available
  to everyone for free. … We didn't know about 'Pathways' until your
  article."

THE FALL
--------
- April 4, 2026: Y Combinator removes Delve from its directory and asks
  the founders to leave the program.
- A leaked internal Bookface chat from YC CEO Garry Tan said: "We have
  asked Delve to leave YC. YC is a community, not just an accelerator.
  The founders in our community have to trust each other, and we have
  to trust them. When that trust breaks down, there's really only one
  thing to do."
- Insight Partners removed (then later partially restored) blog posts
  about the investment from its website. The LinkedIn announcement
  remains deleted.
- COO Kocalar's reaction on X: "YC and Delve have parted ways. I still
  remember the day we took our YC interview at MIT. We're so grateful to
  the community and every founder friend we've made."

DELVE'S DEFENSE
---------------
The founders published a video and blog post, claiming:
- This was a "coordinated cyberattack" — an attacker bought Delve under
  false pretenses, exfiltrated data, and "weaponized it in a smear campaign"
- Two cybersecurity firms had reportedly confirmed this
- DeepDelver "dismisses our AI while acknowledging it automated 70% of a
  security questionnaire" (which, according to critics, just admits the
  fraud — the AI was generating false answers)
- They acknowledged some operational failures: Karun Kaushik on X: "we grew
  too fast and fell short of our own standard. To our customers, we deeply
  apologize for the inconveniences caused."
- Their core defense: "Delve does not itself perform audits or issue final
  SOC 2 reports, but instead provides software and automation." Critics
  noted this defense literally describes the alleged fraud mechanism —
  generating drafts that auditors rubber-stamp.

REACTIONS / TWEETS
------------------
- Aakash Gupta on X: "When your defense to 'you faked the reports' is 'we
  only made the drafts, the auditors signed them,' you've described the
  alleged fraud mechanism in your own press release."
- Gergely Orosz on X: "This Delve story is going from 'very bad' to 'beyond
  very bad.' Apparently Delve's founders were so shameless that they
  1. Charged a fellow YC company (Sim) their full fee for 'auditing' (that
  turned out to be fake), 2. Then ripped off Sim's IP, and sold it to
  customers for $$"
- Barry McCardel on X: "there's something truly sublime about cluely being
  scammed on their SOC 2"
- Jason Calacanis (on his podcast) compared Delve to FTX: "nobody did any
  diligence, and the reason was, that founder, I believe — and then he
  went to jail and was found guilty in the court of law — used other
  people's reputation and the momentum behind crypto to bypass diligence."

QUOTABLE FROM THE SUBSTACK
---------------------------
- "Delve built a machine designed to make clients complicit without their
  knowledge, to manufacture plausible deniability while producing exactly
  the opposite. Delve achieves its claim of being the fastest platform by
  skipping major framework requirements."
- "By generating auditor conclusions, test procedures, and final reports
  before any independent review occurs, Delve places itself in the role
  of both implementer and examiner."
- "Hundreds of companies apparently accepted pre-populated evidence
  without questioning it [which] shows how much the market has prioritized
  speed over control effectiveness."

WHY DELVE IS PERFECT FOR THE 30U30 SIMULATOR
---------------------------------------------
- Both founders are MIT dropouts on Forbes 30 Under 30 list THE SAME YEAR
  it imploded
- Customers may now face HIPAA criminal liability (up to 10 years in
  federal prison) or GDPR fines up to 4% of global revenue
- The "we only made the drafts" defense is literally the joke
- The IP theft from a fellow YC alum is the move that got them killed,
  not the customer fraud — which is itself revealing about the YC ecosystem
- Out-of-home advertising spend in fall 2025: bus ads, billboards across
  SF, NYC, Austin. They published a blog post bragging about spend levels
  and the principle that "if this startup can afford this and pull it off,
  they must be legit"

2. FTX / SAM BANKMAN-FRIED — THE CRYPTO GREEK TRAGEDY (2017–2024)
================================================================================

THE PITCH
---------
Sam Bankman-Fried (SBF) — MIT-educated son of two Stanford law professors —
left Jane Street Capital in 2017 to co-found Alameda Research, then FTX in
2019. The story: he was making money so he could give it all away. He was
the J.P. Morgan of crypto. He was the crypto industry's cleanest, most
politically connected, regulator-friendly face. He was an Effective
Altruist who slept on a beanbag and drove a Toyota Corolla. Forbes had
him at $20+ billion net worth at peak. He was on the 2021 Forbes 30 Under
30 list (Finance category).

THE INNER CIRCLE
----------------
The "polycule" that wasn't quite a polycule but was definitely something:
SBF and 9 colleagues lived together in the $30-40M penthouse "Orchid V" at
Albany Resort in the Bahamas (a 600-acre resort partially owned by Tiger
Woods, Justin Timberlake, Joe Lewis, and Ernie Els). Eight of the 10
housemates were paired off in romantic relationships with each other.

Key roommates / lieutenants:
- Caroline Ellison — CEO of Alameda Research, SBF's on-and-off girlfriend.
  Stanford grad, daughter of two MIT economists. Effective Altruist.
  Maintained a Tumblr ("worldoptimization") with views on polyamory as
  "imperial Chinese harem," eugenics-adjacent ideas, and Harry Potter.
  Pleaded guilty to 7 counts. Sentenced to 2 years (released after 14 months
  in January 2026). Forbes 30 Under 30 (2022) — placed in their "Hall of
  Shame" the next year.
- Gary Wang — Co-founder, CTO. MIT roommate. Pleaded guilty.
- Nishad Singh — Director of Engineering. High school friend of SBF's
  brother. Pleaded guilty. Took a $10M loan from FTX. Testified that he
  was "shocked" by spending on K5 (see below).
- Sam Trabucco — Co-CEO of Alameda with Ellison. Stepped down August 2022.

THE FRAUD MECHANISM (simplified)
--------------------------------
- FTX was the customer-facing exchange. Customer deposits should have stayed
  there.
- Alameda Research, ostensibly a separate hedge fund, had a special "negative
  balance" line of credit through the FTX backend code that Gary Wang wrote.
- Ellison testified SBF directed her to use FTX customer funds to cover
  Alameda losses, repay loans, buy real estate, invest in startups, make
  political donations, and fund celebrity endorsements.
- At collapse, Alameda allegedly owed FTX $10B+ in customer money.

THE SPENDING (via Michael Lewis's "Going Infinite" and trial testimony)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
- Tom Brady: $55M for 20 hours/year for 3 years
- Steph Curry: $35M for 3 years
- Larry David: $10M (the famous "I'm not so sure about this..." Super Bowl ad)
- Gisele Bündchen: undisclosed (named in class action)
- Naomi Osaka, Stephen Curry, Kevin O'Leary: also signed on
- Miami Heat arena naming rights: $135M over 19 years
- Mercedes F1 sponsorship
- Golden State Warriors sponsorship
- MLB umpire uniform logos
- $200M to K5 Global (Michael Kives's company), which networked SBF with
  Hillary Clinton, Doug Emhoff, Katy Perry, Jeff Bezos, Kardashians, etc.
- $74M+ on Bahamian real estate including the $30M penthouse
- $16M waterfront house in the Bahamas for SBF's parents Joe Bankman and
  Barbara Fried (using FTX Trading bank account funds)
- Joe Bankman was on FTX payroll at $200K/year as an "advisor on corporate
  and tax matters." He emailed asking about the salary: "Gee Sam, I don't
  know what to say here. This is the first I have heard of the 200K a year
  salary! Putting Barbara on this."
- $1B personal loan from FTX to SBF
- Massive Democratic political donations (Nishad Singh testified he was a
  straw donor for SBF)

THE COLLAPSE (Nov 2022)
-----------------------
- Nov 2: CoinDesk publishes a leaked Alameda balance sheet showing it was
  largely backed by FTT (FTX's own token).
- Nov 6: Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao tweets that Binance is selling its FTT.
  Bank run begins.
- Nov 8: Binance announces intent to acquire FTX. Within 24 hours, walks away.
- Nov 9: Caroline Ellison admits to Alameda employees on a video call that
  FTX customer funds had been used to cover Alameda liabilities, and that
  she, SBF, Wang, and Singh knew.
- Nov 11: FTX, Alameda, and 130+ entities file Chapter 11. SBF resigns.
  John J. Ray III appointed CEO. Ray is the corporate cleanup guy who ran
  Enron's bankruptcy. His quote: "Never in my career have I seen such a
  complete failure of corporate controls and such a complete absence of
  trustworthy financial information as occurred here."
- Hundreds of millions are siphoned from FTX wallets in "unauthorized
  transactions" — the company says it was "hacked."
- Late Nov: SBF puts the penthouse up for sale at $39.9M.
- Dec 12: Bahamian authorities arrest SBF in the Bahamas.
- Dec 22: Extradited to U.S.

THE TRIAL (Oct-Nov 2023)
-----------------------
- 7 counts: wire fraud (x2), conspiracy to commit wire fraud (x2),
  conspiracy to commit securities fraud, conspiracy to commit commodities
  fraud, conspiracy to commit money laundering.
- Caroline Ellison testified for 3 days. She wept on the stand.
- Gary Wang and Nishad Singh testified.
- SBF's bail was revoked August 2023 after Judge Lewis Kaplan determined he
  tried to intimidate witnesses (he had leaked Ellison's diary to the NYT).
- Prosecutor Danielle Sassoon: "He didn't bargain for his three loyal deputies
  taking that stand and telling you the truth: that he was the one with the
  plan, the motive and the greed to raid FTX customer deposits — billions
  and billions of dollars — to give himself money, power, influence. He
  thought the rules did not apply to him. He thought that he could get
  away with it."
- SBF testified in his own defense. Widely seen as a disaster.
- Convicted on all 7 counts after 3 hours of jury deliberation.

THE SENTENCE (March 28, 2024)
-----------------------------
- 25 years federal prison.
- $11 billion forfeiture.
- Judge Lewis Kaplan: SBF "will be in a position to do something very bad
  in the future, and it's not a trivial risk."
- SBF's allocution: "A lot of people feel really let down, and they were
  very let down, and I am sorry about that. I am sorry about what happened
  at every stage. And there are things I should've done and things I
  shouldn't have."
- He also said: "It haunts me every day."
- Defense had asked for ≤6.5 years. Prosecution had asked for 40-50 years.
- April 2026: Judge Kaplan denies SBF's motion for a new trial.

THE EFFECTIVE ALTRUISM CONNECTION (the underrated piece)
--------------------------------------------------------
SBF rose through the ranks of EA. The whole "earn to give" framework
provided the moral architecture — and the recruiting pipeline — for FTX.
The FTX Future Fund was meant to be the largest EA grantmaker in history.
When FTX collapsed, the Future Fund's senior staff resigned, citing
"inability to honor grants and concerns about the legitimacy of its funding."
Many EA orgs that had received FTX money were asked to consider returning
it (clawback risk).

Quotable from Michael Lewis on 60 Minutes:
"He committed to making as much money as possible… so he could give it
away as efficiently as possible…guided by a social movement, effective
altruism. … You don't understand Sam Bankman-Fried unless you understand
that he turns everything into a game."
"Every cause he sought to serve, he damaged. Every cause he sought to fight,
he helped. He was a person who set out in life to maximize the consequences
of his actions — never mind the intent. And he had exactly the opposite
effects of the ones he set out to have. So it looks to me like his life is
a cruel joke."

LIFESTYLE DISSONANCE (great for satire)
---------------------------------------
- Drove a Toyota Corolla
- Wore cargo shorts and rumpled t-shirts on stage with Bill Clinton, Tony Blair
- Slept on a beanbag in the office
- Penthouse freezer stocked with $2.99 Trader Joe's microwavable veg biryani,
  flown in from Miami
- Allegedly tried to bribe Donald Trump $5B not to run again for president
  (per Going Infinite — Trump's team said no, and floated $1B)
- Listen-in plus text-coaching while another exec impersonated a YouTube exec
  was Ozy, not FTX — but FTX had its own version: SBF gave Anthony Scaramucci
  a 30% stake in his company at FTX's behest

3. THERANOS / ELIZABETH HOLMES — PATIENT ZERO (2003–2024)
================================================================================

THE PITCH
---------
Elizabeth Holmes dropped out of Stanford at 19 in 2003 to start a company
that would revolutionize blood testing. The promise: a single drop of blood
from a fingerprick could run hundreds of medical diagnostics on a tabletop
device called the Edison. The company eventually claimed it had partnerships
with the U.S. military (medevac helicopters in Afghanistan), Walgreens
(8,000 store rollout planned), and Safeway (which actually remodeled stores
in anticipation).

At peak (2014-2015), Theranos was valued at $9 billion and Holmes was the
youngest self-made female billionaire on Forbes (paper-net-worth $4.5B).
She was on the cover of Fortune, Forbes, Inc., T Magazine.

THE AESTHETIC (heavily borrowed from Steve Jobs)
------------------------------------------------
- Black turtleneck, every day, deliberately
- Famously dropped her voice an octave for public appearances
- "What is the alternative? You die."
- Partnered with Carlos Slim to "improve blood testing in Mexico"
- Helped pass an Arizona law in 2015 letting people get lab tests without
  insurance or doctor approval — while misrepresenting Theranos's accuracy

THE BOARD (the most intentionally credentialed in startup history)
------------------------------------------------------------------
Holmes deliberately stocked the board with names that signaled trust without
contributing technical scrutiny:
- Henry Kissinger — Former U.S. Secretary of State
- George Shultz — Former Secretary of State and Treasury (also Tyler Shultz's
  grandfather, which would matter)
- James Mattis — Retired four-star Marine Corps General, future SecDef.
  Personally invested $85,000.
- William Perry — Former U.S. Secretary of Defense
- Sam Nunn — Former U.S. Senator
- Bill Frist — Former Senate Majority Leader, doctor
- Richard Kovacevich — Former Wells Fargo CEO
- Dick Costolo — Former Twitter CEO (joined a tech advisory board later)
- Channing Robertson — Stanford chemical engineering professor, Holmes's
  first faculty advisor and earliest cheerleader

THE INVESTORS
-------------
- The Walton family (Walmart heirs) — $150M
- Rupert Murdoch — $125M
- Betsy DeVos's family — $100M
- Larry Ellison
- Tim Draper (DFJ) — Holmes's neighbor as a kid; doubled down even after
  the fraud was exposed
- Total: ~$945M raised

WHAT THE TECHNOLOGY ACTUALLY WAS
--------------------------------
- The Edison machine couldn't reliably do most of the tests it claimed
- For commercial tests, Theranos secretly used commercially-available
  third-party blood analyzers, often diluting samples to make finger-prick
  volumes work, which introduced massive error
- Internal: employees quietly deleted erroneous test results
- A 2015 CMS audit found "the deficient practices of the laboratory pose
  immediate jeopardy to patient health and safety"

THE WHISTLEBLOWERS
-------------------
- Tyler Shultz — George Shultz's 22-year-old grandson. Worked at Theranos
  for 8 months in 2013-2014 on the assay validation team. Reported quality
  control failures internally and was met with hostility. Resigned. Blew
  the whistle to NY state regulators using an alias. Became central
  anonymous source for John Carreyrou.
  - Theranos lawyers tried to coerce him to sign affidavits naming other
    whistleblowers. His grandfather invited him over under false pretenses
    with Theranos lawyers waiting upstairs. Tyler walked out.
  - His parents spent $400,000–$500,000 on his legal fees.
  - He believed he was being followed by Theranos's private investigators
    for over a year.
  - Bought a burner phone with cash to call Carreyrou.
  - He and his grandfather did eventually reconcile before George Shultz's death.
  - Tyler was on Forbes 30 Under 30 (2017, Healthcare).
- Erika Cheung — Theranos lab tech and Tyler's friend. Reported to CMS.
  Her report subsequently led to the shutdown of Theranos's clinical lab.
  Co-founded Ethics in Entrepreneurship. Testified at Holmes's trial.
- Adam Rosendorff — Former Theranos lab director. Critical source.

THE JOURNALIST
--------------
John Carreyrou — Wall Street Journal investigative reporter. Won 2 Pulitzers.
First WSJ story published October 15, 2015. His book "Bad Blood: Secrets and
Lies in a Silicon Valley Startup" (2018) is the genre-defining text. He's
the patron saint of startup-fraud journalism.

Theranos hired David Boies (the genuinely scary lawyer) to threaten Carreyrou
and the WSJ. They spied on Carreyrou. They threatened Tyler Shultz's family.
None of it worked.

THE FALL
--------
- Oct 2015: WSJ first story
- 2016: CMS sanctions, Walgreens shuts 40 testing centers, sues
- 2018: SEC charges Holmes and COO Sunny Balwani with raising $700M through
  fraud. Holmes settles civil charges with $500K fine, returns 18.9M shares,
  10-year ban from being officer/director of public company.
- June 2018: Federal grand jury indicts Holmes and Balwani (criminal)
- Holmes is engaged/married to William "Billy" Evans (a hotel heir),
  has 2 kids during the legal proceedings.
- Trials separated due to Holmes's allegations of abuse by Balwani
- Jan 2022: Holmes convicted on 4 counts of investor fraud (acquitted on
  patient fraud counts)
- July 2022: Balwani convicted on all counts
- Nov 18, 2022: Holmes sentenced to 11 years 3 months
- Dec 7, 2022: Balwani sentenced to 12 years 11 months
- Both ordered to pay $452M restitution jointly. $125M to Murdoch.
- May 30, 2023: Holmes reports to Federal Prison Camp Bryan, Texas
- Feb 2025: 9th Circuit upholds convictions
- Dec 2025: LA Times reports Holmes is campaigning for a Trump pardon

THE SECOND-IN-COMMAND
----------------------
Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani — Pakistani-Indian software exec, multimillionaire
from the dot-com era. Met Holmes when she was 18 (he was 37). Became her
secret romantic partner and Theranos COO/President. Trial revealed his
WhatsApp/text response to Tyler Shultz's email about quality control:
"Before I get into specifics, let me share with you that had this email
come from anyone else in the company, I would have already held them
accountable for the arrogant and patronizing tone and reckless comments."

11. CLUELY / ROY LEE — FRAUD-AS-MARKETING ERA (2025-)

THE PITCH
---------
Chungin "Roy" Lee, 21, Columbia University CS student. He and Neel Shanmugam
built "Interview Coder," a desktop AI tool that runs invisibly in a hidden
browser window during virtual technical interviews and feeds the candidate
real-time answers. Lee used it to land an Amazon internship and posted on X
about it. Amazon rescinded the offer. Columbia put him on academic probation,
then suspended him.

April 2025: Rebranded to Cluely. Slogan: "cheat on everything." Launch video
shows Lee using Cluely on a date to lie about his age and pretend to know
about art. The video was widely compared to a Black Mirror episode.

THE MONEY
---------
- April 2025: $5.3M seed (Abstract Ventures, Susa Ventures)
- June 2025: $15M Series A led by Andreessen Horowitz
- Total: $20.3M

Marc Andreessen — yes, that one — backed Cluely.

THE LIVING SITUATION
--------------------
SoMa office/house. 16 employees, mostly college dropouts. The CEO's nameplate
is a handwritten "CEO Cluely" taped to the wall. Self-described as "as fratty
as possible." Quote from Lee: "I always harken back to ancestral living. Humans
are biologically primed to live in a group, hunt with each other, and work
together. We wake up, and we're all excited to hunt down the king of the
jungle."

One employee is an 18-year-old high school non-graduate from Minnetonka MN
who left without a diploma. Another is a 40-year-old engineering intern.

THE STRATEGY
------------
Lee says viral controversy is the actual product strategy. He pays "over 60
content creators and 700 video editors" to flood social media. At TechCrunch
Disrupt 2025: "Engineers just cannot make good content."

THE LIE
-------
March 2026: Lee admits on X that the company's previously reported "$7M ARR"
figure (cited in the SF Standard profile and elsewhere) was not accurate.
Calls it "the only blatantly dishonest thing I've said publicly online."

This of course is funny because the entire company is built on dishonesty
in interviews and meetings.

ALSO WORTH NOTING
-----------------
Cluely was a Delve customer. Barry McCardel on X: "there's something truly
sublime about cluely being scammed on their SOC 2." (See Section 1.)

By June 2025, all references to "cheating on job interviews" had been
removed from Cluely's marketing site.

13. RECURRING TROPES & PATTERNS — FOR EVENT DESIGN
================================================================================

These are the BEHAVIORAL ARCHETYPES that show up across every fraud. Each
one is a potential event template for the simulator.

A. THE METRIC INFLATION
-----------------------
Every single major fraud case involved inflating one core metric:
- HeadSpin: ARR ($26M → $95M reported)
- Frank: users (300K → 4M+)
- IRL: users (5% real, 95% bots)
- Outcome Health: screen installations
- Theranos: number of tests their machines could perform / accuracy
- Cluely: ARR ($7M was a lie)
- FTX: customer asset segregation (it wasn't)
- Nikola: vehicle capability + production numbers

EVENT TEMPLATE: "A key metric is below target before fundraising. Choices:
A) lower the target B) hire a 'data scientist' to 'clean up' the data
C) just inflate it"

B. THE PRESSURED FRAUD ASK TO EMPLOYEE
---------------------------------------
- Javice → Patrick Vovor: "Generate synthetic user data" (he refused)
- Shafi → engineers: hide ad spend through third-party invoicing
- Lachwani → employees: include revenue from prospects who didn't sign
- Outcome Health: false billing reports
- Theranos: delete erroneous test results

The employee who refuses becomes the witness. The employee who agrees
becomes the cooperating witness.

C. THE OVERCREDENTIALED BOARD
-----------------------------
- Theranos: Kissinger, Mattis, Shultz, Perry, Nunn, Frist
- Common pattern: stack the board with retired generals, ex-cabinet
  secretaries, ex-CEOs to signal trust without creating actual oversight
- Used to override skepticism: "are you really going to question
  Henry Kissinger's company?"

D. THE TWO-COMPANY STRUCTURE / RELATED-PARTY STRUCTURE
------------------------------------------------------
- FTX/Alameda: The fraud was made possible by the ostensible separation
  of the exchange and the trading firm
- WeWork: Adam Neumann owned buildings, leased them to WeWork, owned the
  "We" trademark
- Outcome Health: founders gave themselves $225M dividends out of
  fraud-inflated equity raises

E. THE DUE DILIGENCE FAILURE
-----------------------------
The sucker isn't always who you think. JPMorgan had ~300 in-house diligence
officers vet Frank. SoftBank decided in 12 minutes. Tiger Global, Insight
Partners, Goldman Sachs all got smoked. The fundraising environment
matters: when capital is plentiful, diligence drops. When everyone
wants to lead, FOMO replaces analysis.

F. THE WHISTLEBLOWER RETALIATION CYCLE
--------------------------------------
- Theranos: PIs followed Tyler Shultz; pressured him to sign affidavits;
  threatened lawsuits; David Boies sent threats to the WSJ
- IRL: fired employees who raised bot concerns; Nicholas Grant filed a
  retaliation complaint
- Outcome Health: a new C-suite hire raised alarms within weeks, lasted
  3 weeks
- Javice: Patrick Vovor's character was attacked at trial — defense
  suggested he was resentful Javice didn't date him

G. THE LIFESTYLE GIVEAWAY
--------------------------
The frauds often spend in revealing ways:
- Holmes: black turtlenecks (cosplay)
- SBF: Toyota Corolla and beanbag (anti-cosplay) but with a $30M penthouse
- Neumann: $60M jet, building purchases
- Shafi: company credit cards on wedding, art classes, clothing
- WeWork: ball pits, in-office bars, $36K/year primary school

H. THE PERFORMATIVE MORAL FRAMING
---------------------------------
- Holmes: revolutionizing healthcare, helping millions
- SBF: effective altruism, save humanity from extinction
- Neumann: "elevate the world's consciousness"
- Javice: "help students get aid"
- Watson: representing Black voices in media
- Shafi: organic community building

The moral framing is itself the alibi.

I. THE SHORT-SELLER OR JOURNALIST CATALYST
-------------------------------------------
- Theranos: Carreyrou / WSJ
- Nikola: Hindenburg Research
- Outcome Health: WSJ
- Ozy: Ben Smith / NYT
- Delve: anonymous Substack (DeepDelver)
- IRL: The Information (Cory Weinberg)
- HeadSpin: The Information

The catalyst event is publication. The post-publication 72 hours is the
phase change.

J. THE GENERIC CONFESSION
-------------------------
Compare:
- SBF: "I made a number of small mistakes and a number of larger mistakes."
- Javice: "I did something that runs against the grain of my upbringing.
  I made choices that I will spend my entire life regretting."
- Holmes: "I am devastated by my failings."
- Shafi: pleads not guilty
- Lee: "the only blatantly dishonest thing I've said publicly"

Notice how it's always "mistakes" or "choices" or "failings" — never "fraud"
or "lies." The vocabulary of crisis comms.

K. THE LONG TAIL LEGAL FIGHT
-----------------------------
After conviction:
- Holmes: 9th Circuit appeal (denied 2025); now campaigning for Trump pardon
- SBF: motion for new trial (denied April 2026)
- Shah: bail pending appeal
- Javice: out on $2M bail, pending appeal, fighting ankle bracelet, teaching Pilates
